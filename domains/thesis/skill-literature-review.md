# Literature Review Domain

Literature review best practices for academic papers.

## When to Activate

Activate when conducting literature reviews, synthesizing research, or identifying research gaps.

## Core Principles

1. **Be Comprehensive** — Cover relevant literature thoroughly
2. **Be Critical** — Evaluate sources critically
3. **Be Organized** — Organize logically
4. **Be Current** — Use current sources

## Literature Search

### Databases

**General databases:**
- Google Scholar — Broad coverage, free access
- Web of Science — Comprehensive, citation tracking
- Scopus — Large database, citation analysis
- Microsoft Academic — Free, comprehensive

**Field-specific databases:**
- PubMed — Biomedical literature
- IEEE Xplore — Engineering and computer science
- ACM Digital Library — Computer science
- arXiv — Preprints in physics, CS, math, etc.
- SSRN — Social sciences research network
- JSTOR — Humanities and social sciences

**Example:**
> **Database Selection Strategy:**
> - Start with Google Scholar for broad overview
> - Use field-specific databases for depth
> - Check recent arXiv preprints for latest work
> - Use citation chaining to find related work

### Search Strategies

**Use keywords:**
- Identify key terms
- Use synonyms and variations
- Include technical terms
- Consider alternative spellings

**Use Boolean operators:**
- AND: narrows search (machine AND learning)
- OR: broadens search (neural OR deep)
- NOT: excludes terms (learning NOT machine)
- Use parentheses for grouping

**Use citation chaining:**
- Forward chaining: papers citing your key papers
- Backward chaining: papers cited by your key papers
- Both directions for comprehensive coverage
- Use citation databases for efficiency

**Use author searches:**
- Key authors in your field
- Recent publications by key authors
- Collaboration networks
- Author profiles

**Example:**
> **Search Query Example:**
> ("machine learning" OR "deep learning") AND ("medical text" OR "clinical text") AND ("classification" OR "categorization") AND (transformer OR "attention mechanism")

### Source Evaluation

**Check credibility:**
- Peer-reviewed journals preferred
- Reputable conferences
- Established publishers
- Institutional repositories

**Check relevance:**
- Directly relevant to your topic
- Addresses similar questions
- Uses similar methods
- Provides useful insights

**Check recency:**
- Recent work (last 5-10 years)
- Seminal papers (older but important)
- Balance between recent and foundational
- Check for follow-up work

**Check methodology:**
- Sound methodology
- Adequate sample size
- Appropriate analysis
- Reproducible results

**Example:**
> **Source Evaluation Criteria:**
> - Peer-reviewed: Yes/No
> - Relevance: High/Medium/Low
> - Recency: Year published
> - Methodology: Sound/Questionable
> - Citations: Number and quality

## Literature Synthesis

### Thematic Organization

**Organize by theme:**
- Identify common themes
- Group related studies
- Create thematic sections
- Use clear headings

**Group related studies:**
- Similar methodologies
- Similar findings
- Similar research questions
- Similar theoretical frameworks

**Identify patterns:**
- Common findings across studies
- Methodological trends
- Theoretical developments
- Research directions

**Highlight trends:**
- Historical development
- Current state of field
- Emerging trends
- Future directions

**Example:**
> **Thematic Organization:**
> 1. Early approaches to medical NLP
> 2. Deep learning in medical text analysis
> 3. Transformer architectures for clinical text
> 4. Domain adaptation techniques
> 5. Current challenges and future directions

### Critical Analysis

**Evaluate methodologies:**
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Appropriateness for research question
- Sample size and representativeness
- Data quality and availability

**Compare findings:**
- Consistent findings
- Contradictory results
- Possible explanations for differences
- Need for further research

**Identify contradictions:**
- Conflicting results
- Different interpretations
- Methodological differences
- Contextual factors

**Note limitations:**
- Study limitations
- Methodological constraints
- Sample limitations
- Generalizability concerns

**Example:**
> **Critical Analysis Example:**
> "While Smith et al. (2020) reported 95% accuracy using their approach, Jones et al. (2021) found only 85% accuracy with similar methods. This discrepancy may be due to differences in dataset characteristics: Smith used curated clinical notes, while Jones used noisy social media posts."

### Gap Identification

**Identify research gaps:**
- What hasn't been studied
- What needs further investigation
- What questions remain unanswered
- What methods need improvement

**Note unanswered questions:**
- Questions not addressed
- Partially answered questions
- Questions with conflicting answers
- New questions raised

**Highlight opportunities:**
- Promising research directions
- Methodological improvements
- New applications
- Interdisciplinary opportunities

**Propose directions:**
- Specific research questions
- Methodological approaches
- Data needs
- Evaluation strategies

**Example:**
> **Research Gap Identification:**
> "Despite extensive work on general-domain text classification, medical text classification remains challenging due to domain-specific terminology. Existing approaches either require large amounts of labeled medical data or fail to capture domain knowledge effectively. This gap motivates our work on domain-adapted transformers with medical terminology embeddings."

## Best Practices

### Search

**Use multiple databases:**
- Start broad, then narrow
- Use field-specific databases
- Check multiple sources
- Use citation databases

**Use systematic approach:**
- Document search strategy
- Use consistent terminology
- Record search results
- Track search history

**Document search strategy:**
- Keywords used
- Databases searched
- Date ranges
- Inclusion/exclusion criteria

**Save search results:**
- Export citations
- Organize by theme
- Add notes
- Use reference manager

### Evaluation

**Evaluate sources critically:**
- Check methodology
- Assess sample size
- Consider bias
- Evaluate conclusions

**Note methodological limitations:**
- Study design limitations
- Sample limitations
- Measurement limitations
- Analysis limitations

**Consider sample sizes:**
- Adequate for conclusions
- Representative of population
- Statistical power
- Generalizability

**Check for bias:**
- Selection bias
- Publication bias
- Confirmation bias
- Funding bias

### Synthesis

**Synthesize findings:**
- Identify common themes
- Compare and contrast
- Note contradictions
- Highlight patterns

**Identify patterns:**
- Methodological patterns
- Theoretical patterns
- Empirical patterns
- Temporal patterns

**Note contradictions:**
- Conflicting results
- Different interpretations
- Methodological differences
- Contextual factors

**Highlight gaps:**
- What's missing
- What needs more work
- What's unclear
- What's controversial

### Writing

**Organize logically:**
- Clear structure
- Logical flow
- Thematic organization
- Clear transitions

**Use clear transitions:**
- Between paragraphs
- Between sections
- Between themes
- Between ideas

**Cite appropriately:**
- All claims supported
- Proper citation format
- Consistent style
- Complete references

**Avoid plagiarism:**
- Paraphrase properly
- Quote when necessary
- Cite all sources
- Use your own words

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Incomplete coverage** — Be comprehensive
2. **Lack of critical analysis** — Evaluate sources critically
3. **Poor organization** — Organize logically
4. **Outdated sources** — Use current sources
5. **Missing synthesis** — Synthesize, don't just list
6. **No gap identification** — Identify research gaps
7. **Poor citation** — Cite appropriately
8. **Plagiarism** — Avoid plagiarism
