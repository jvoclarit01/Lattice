# Abstract Writing Domain

Abstract writing best practices for academic papers.

## When to Activate

Activate when writing abstracts, summarizing research, or creating executive summaries.

## Core Principles

1. **Be Concise** — Keep it brief and to the point
2. **Be Complete** — Cover all key points without omission
3. **Be Clear** — Be clear and understandable to your audience
4. **Be Accurate** — Be accurate and honest about findings

## Abstract Structure

### Background (1-2 sentences)
- Brief context of the research field
- Clearly state the research problem
- Identify the research gap
- Explain why this problem matters

**Example:**
> "Natural language processing has made significant advances in text classification, yet existing models struggle with domain-specific terminology in medical literature. This gap limits the effectiveness of automated literature review systems in healthcare research."

### Methods (2-3 sentences)
- State your research approach clearly
- Describe your methodology concisely
- Identify data sources and sample size
- Mention key analysis techniques

**Example:**
> "We developed a transformer-based model fine-tuned on 50,000 medical abstracts from PubMed. Our approach incorporates domain-specific embeddings and multi-task learning to capture both semantic and syntactic patterns. We evaluated performance using 5-fold cross-validation on a held-out test set."

### Results (2-3 sentences)
- Present key findings with specific metrics
- Include statistical significance where relevant
- Highlight main outcomes
- Note any unexpected or counterintuitive results

**Example:**
> "Our model achieved 94.2% F1 score, outperforming BERT by 12.3 percentage points (p < 0.001). The domain-specific embeddings contributed 4.7 points to overall performance. Notably, performance improved with document length, contrary to initial expectations."

### Conclusion (1-2 sentences)
- State the implications of your findings
- Summarize your main contributions
- Acknowledge key limitations
- Suggest future directions

**Example:**
> "These results demonstrate the value of domain-adapted language models for medical text processing. Our approach enables more accurate automated literature review, though generalization to other domains requires further investigation."

## Abstract Types

### Descriptive Abstract
- Describes what the paper covers
- Does not include results or conclusions
- Short length: 100-150 words
- Best for: Review papers, methodology papers

**Example:**
> "This paper reviews recent advances in transformer architectures for natural language processing. We examine key architectural innovations, training methodologies, and application domains. The review covers developments from 2017 to present, with emphasis on efficiency improvements and scaling laws."

### Informative Abstract
- Includes results and conclusions
- More detailed than descriptive
- Standard length: 150-250 words
- Best for: Research papers, empirical studies

### Structured Abstract
- Uses labeled sections (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusion)
- Clear organization for easy scanning
- Standard format for medical journals
- Often required by specific journals

**Example:**
> **Background:** Climate change poses significant threats to coastal ecosystems...
> **Methods:** We surveyed 47 coastal sites across three continents...
> **Results:** Species richness declined by 23% in vulnerable areas...
> **Conclusion:** Immediate conservation action is required...

## Best Practices

### Content Guidelines

**DO:**
- Cover all key points from the paper
- Be specific about your findings
- Use concrete numbers and metrics
- Include statistical significance when relevant
- State your main contribution clearly

**DON'T:**
- Use vague or ambiguous language
- Include information not in the paper
- Overstate your findings
- Use undefined abbreviations
- Repeat the title verbatim

### Style Guidelines

**Language:**
- Use clear, accessible language
- Avoid unnecessary jargon
- Define essential technical terms
- Use active voice for stronger writing
- Maintain consistent terminology

**Example of passive vs. active:**
- Passive: "It was found that..."
- Active: "We found that..." or "Our analysis revealed..."

**Tense Usage:**
- Background: Present tense ("This problem affects...")
- Methods: Past tense ("We collected data from...")
- Results: Past tense ("Results showed that...")
- Conclusion: Present tense ("These findings suggest...")

### Length Management

**Word Count Targets:**
- Conference abstract: 150-250 words
- Journal abstract: 200-300 words
- Thesis abstract: 300-500 words
- Extended abstract: 500-1000 words

**Editing Strategies:**
1. Remove redundant phrases ("very", "really", "quite")
2. Combine related sentences
3. Delete unnecessary background
4. Focus on unique contributions
5. Cut methods to essentials

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Too much background** — Keep context minimal, focus on your work
2. **Vague results** — Include specific numbers and metrics
3. **Missing conclusions** — Always state the implications
4. **New information** — Don't introduce concepts not in the paper
5. **Overpromising** — Be realistic about your contributions
6. **Undefined acronyms** — Spell out on first use
7. **Citations in abstract** — Generally avoid, unless required

## Abstract Templates

### Template 1: Empirical Study
```
[Problem statement] remains a significant challenge in [field]. This study addresses [specific gap] by [approach]. Using [methodology] with [data description], we [what you did]. Results show [key findings with metrics], demonstrating [significance]. These findings contribute to [field] by [contribution], suggesting [implications].
```

### Template 2: Methodological Paper
```
Existing methods for [task] face limitations including [specific problems]. We propose [method name], a novel approach that [key innovation]. Our method [how it works] and [key advantages]. Evaluation on [datasets] shows [performance metrics], outperforming [baselines] by [margin]. This approach enables [new capabilities] for [applications].
```

### Template 3: Review/Survey Paper
```
[Field] has experienced rapid growth in [time period], with advances in [key areas]. This review synthesizes [scope] research from [sources], examining [themes]. We identify [key trends] and highlight [important findings]. Critical gaps remain in [areas], suggesting future research should focus on [directions]. This review provides [value] for [audience].
```

## Evaluation Checklist

Before submitting your abstract, verify:

- [ ] Word count meets journal/conference requirements
- [ ] All key sections are present (background, methods, results, conclusion)
- [ ] Results include specific metrics and statistical significance
- [ ] No undefined abbreviations or acronyms
- [ ] No citations (unless required)
- [ ] No information not present in the full paper
- [ ] Active voice used throughout
- [ ] Consistent terminology with the full paper
- [ ] Clear statement of contribution
- [ ] Appropriate tense usage
